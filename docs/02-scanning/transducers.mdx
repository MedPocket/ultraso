Selecting the correct transducer and masterfully controlling its orientation on the patient's skin are the absolute pre-requisites for diagnostic-quality imaging.

## Transducer Families

Clinical imaging relies primarily on three types of transducers, each optimized for different physical frequencies and footprint geometry.

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="Linear Transducer" icon="minus">
    High frequency (7-15+ MHz). Flat surface. Excellent near-field resolution, shallow penetration. Ideal for vascular access, thyroid, MSK, and lung sliding.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Curvilinear Transducer" icon="unfold-horizontal">
    Low frequency (2-5 MHz). Curved surface. Wide field of view, deep penetration. Ideal for abdominal (FAST exam), obstetrics, and pelvic examinations.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Phased Array Transducer" icon="grid">
    Low frequency (1-5 MHz). Tiny footprint. Flat beam that sweeps dynamically. Ideal for scanning between narrow ribs (Cardiac / Transthoracic).
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Standardization and Probe Orientation

Every probe has a built-in **orientation marker** (usually a physical ridge, light, or groove on one side).
On the screen, there is a matching **screen icon** (often a brand logo or green dot).

:::danger[Crucial Rule of Orientation]
Always align the physical orientation marker on the probe with the corresponding marker on the screen!
* **Transverse Scan:** The probe marker should point to the **patient's right**.
* **Sagittal/Coronal Scan:** The probe marker should point towards the **patient's head (cephalad)**.
:::

## Standard Probe Movements

Master these five dynamic movements to optimize your acoustic window and visualize organs fully.

<Steps>
  <Step title="1. Sliding">
    Moving the entire probe footprint across the skin surface in any direction without changing its angle. Use this to travel along a structure (like tracking a blood vessel).
  </Step>
  <Step title="2. Tilting (Fanning)">
    Keeping the footprint in one stationary place but rocking the probe perpendicular to its long axis. This acts like slicing through a loaf of bread, showing consecutive cross-sections of an organ.
  </Step>
  <Step title="3. Rocking (Pitching)">
    Keeping the footprint stationary but tilting the probe along its longitudinal axis. This allows centering of a target or tracking curved structures.
  </Step>
  <Step title="4. Rotating">
    Spinning the probe in place around its central axis. Used to switch immediately between transverse (short axis) and longitudinal (long axis) views.
  </Step>
  <Step title="5. Compression">
    Applying downward pressure. Incredibly important for verifying deep structures, dispersing bowel gas, or testing vessel collapsibility (such as in DVT assessment).
  </Step>
</Steps>
