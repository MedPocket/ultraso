title: Imaging Modes
description: Explore the different display modes used in clinical diagnostic ultrasound.
---

Ultrasound machines process returning echo signals and display them in several primary modes. Choosing the right mode is essential for clinical decision-making.

## Primary Imaging Modes

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="A-Mode (Amplitude)">
    **A-mode** is the simplest type of ultrasound. It displays the amplitude of returning echos as vertical spikes on a 1D graph against depth (time).

    * **Clinical Use:** Primarily used in ophthalmology to measure distances within the eye (e.g., axial length).

  </Tab>
  <Tab title="B-Mode (Brightness)">
    **B-mode** is the foundation of 2D clinical ultrasound. It converts the amplitude of returning echoes into pixels of varying brightness.

    * **High Amplitude Echo (Strong Reflection):** White pixels (**Hyperechoic** - e.g., bone, stones, air).
    * **Low Amplitude Echo (Weak Reflection):** Grey pixels (**Hypoechoic** - e.g., muscle, parenchymal organs).
    * **No Echo (No Reflection/Transmission):** Black pixels (**Anechoic** - e.g., fluid, blood, simple cysts).

  </Tab>
  <Tab title="M-Mode (Motion)">
    **M-mode** samples a single narrow beam line over time. It registers motion of moving structures along that line, plotting it against time.

    * **Clinical Use:** Extremely high temporal resolution. Perfect for assessing moving structures like heart valve leaflets, fetal heart rates, or lung sliding (pneumothorax assessment).

  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Doppler Modes">
    **Doppler** modes detect moving targets (usually red blood cells) by analyzing frequency shifts in the returning echoes.

    * **Color Doppler:** Shows direction and velocity of flow relative to the probe (Red is moving *towards* probe, Blue is *away* - **BART**).
    * **Spectral Doppler:** Graphs the exact velocities of blood flow over the cardiac cycle.
    * **Power Doppler:** Highly sensitive to flow presence but does not show direction. Perfect for low-flow states.

  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Echogenicity Glossary

Understanding these descriptive terms is vital for charting and communicating ultrasound findings:

- **Anechoic:** Complete absence of echoes; appears completely black (e.g., gallbladder lumen, urinary bladder, simple fluid collections).
- **Hypoechoic:** Fewer/weaker echoes than surrounding tissues; appears dark grey (e.g., lymph nodes, standard tumors, renal cortex relative to liver).
- **Isoechoic:** Similar echo density to surrounding tissues; appears matching grey (e.g., some liver lesions or thyroid nodules).
- **Hyperechoic:** Strong reflections; appears bright white (e.g., renal sinus, gallstones, bone surface).

:::note
Echogenicity is always relative to surrounding tissues!
:::
